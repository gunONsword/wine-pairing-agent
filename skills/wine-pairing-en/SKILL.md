# Wine Pairing — English Abstract Knowledge Base

This skill is the English consolidation of Pattern 4 (abstract-to-abstract) food↔wine pairing knowledge gathered across five languages. It is designed to be loaded by an AI agent that needs to (a) reason about pairings from first principles, (b) recommend a wine style for an unknown dish, (c) recommend a dish family for a given wine, or (d) pre-filter candidates before consulting a concrete recipe-to-wine database.

---

## 1. Core philosophy

Two everything-else-follows truths from every source:

- The point of a pairing is **balance** — neither wine nor dish overpowers the other; ideally each makes the other taste better.
- The "perfect pairing" is a fiction; what is reliable is the *direction* the principles below send you.

A working test: taste the wine, taste the dish, taste the wine again. If wine, dish, and wine again all hold up, the pairing works.

## 2. The two strategies

| Strategy | Idea | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| **Concordance / similarity** | Match characteristics (rich + rich, sweet + sweet, herbaceous + herbaceous, smoky + smoky). | You want to confirm and amplify the dominant flavour. |
| **Contrast / opposition** | Oppose characteristics (acid cuts fat; sweet cuts heat; tannin cuts protein/fat). | You want to balance an extreme element (too rich, too spicy, too fatty). |

Both are valid. Most thoughtful pairings use one explicitly.

## 3. The universal rules

### 3.1 Intensity / weight rule
- The wine's weight (body) and the dish's heaviness must match.
- Light dish → light, low-alcohol, fresh, fruity wine.
- Heavy / rich dish → full-bodied, possibly tannic or oaked wine.
- This is the single most-cited rule across all sources.

### 3.2 Acidity rule
- The wine should have **at least as much acidity** as the dish, otherwise it tastes flat or washed out.
- High-acid wines (Sauvignon Blanc, Riesling, Albariño, Champagne) cleanse the palate against fat, cream, frying, oil.
- Always raise wine acidity to meet acid in the dish (tomato, vinaigrette, citrus, raw fish, vinegar).

### 3.3 Sweetness rule
- For desserts, **the wine must be sweeter than the dessert**, or the dessert strips the wine and the wine reads as bitter or sour.
- Dry sparkling (Brut / Extra Brut) + sweet dessert is the most common avoidable mismatch.
- Light sweetness in a savoury dish (fruit sauce over pork) can be met by a rich white whose alcohol reads as sweetness, like an oaked Chardonnay.

### 3.4 Tannin rule
- Tannins bind to protein and fat, softening in the mouth.
- High-tannin reds want red meat, game, mature hard cheese, succulent dishes.
- Avoid high tannin with delicate fish, light vegetables, or bitter foods (tannin amplifies bitterness; bitter + bitter compound, they do not cancel).

### 3.5 Salt rule
- Salt distorts oaky whites, strips fruit from reds, and turns high-alcohol wines bitter.
- Two reliable rescues:
  - Sweet wine + salty food (Sauternes + blue cheese; Port + Stilton).
  - Sparkling + salty fried (carbonation and yeasty acids behave like beer; reset the palate).
- Briny seafood (oysters): high-acid wines clean the salt while balancing the ocean character.

### 3.6 Alcohol rule
- High-alcohol wine + spicy/chilli food amplifies the heat. Avoid.
- Save higher-alcohol wines for braises, stews, steaks.

### 3.7 Sauce / preparation rule
- Pair to the **sauce and the cooking method**, not just the protein.
- Same chicken, three sauces → three different wines.
- Grilled / barbecued / smoked → smoky, mineral, or fumé wines (Rioja, Pinot Noir, Syrah).
- Braised, mijoté, slow-cooked → fresh, acidic wines that lift the dish.
- Boiled, poached, steamed → light reds (Gamay, Riesling-Sylvaner) or crisp simple whites.
- Cooked à feu vif (sharp crust, intense aromas) → tannic reds (Chianti, Barolo).

### 3.8 "What grows together, goes together"
- Regional cuisine + regional wine is rarely wrong.
- Barolo + ossobuco; Pizzoccheri + Valtellina Superiore; Boeuf Bourguignon + Pinot Noir; Rioja + Castilian lamb; Chianti + Tuscan beef; Albariño + Galician seafood.

### 3.9 Service order in a multi-wine meal
- Light before heavy. Young before old. Cool before tempered. Dry before sweet.
- "White before red" is not actually a rule — weight is.

### 3.10 Myths to discard
- ❌ "Red with meat, white with fish." Depends on cut, preparation, and sauce. A swordfish in tomato can take a soft red; chicken in cream wants a white.
- ❌ "Rosé is a hybrid that fits everywhere." Rosé is its own category with its own pairings; it isn't a get-out-of-jail card.
- ❌ "Sparkling pairs with everything." Dry sparkling fails against sweet desserts.
- ❌ "Only sommeliers know the right pairings." Pairing is a literacy, not a credential; the principles in this skill are enough.

## 4. The six elements (Wine Enthusiast framework)

Use these as a checklist when analysing any dish.

**Fat** — wine has none. Balance fat with acid, cut it with tannin, or match its richness with alcohol. Example: steak + Cabernet; fried chicken + Champagne; pork belly + Riesling.

**Acid** — pick wine at least as acidic as the dish. Example: tomato + Chianti; goat cheese + Sauvignon Blanc.

**Salt** — drives toward sweet wine (Sauternes + blue cheese) or sparkling (Champagne + salty fried).

**Sweetness** — match degree. Sweeter dessert needs sweeter wine. Bitter dark chocolate + late-harvest Zinfandel works; sweet chocolate cake + dry red does not.

**Bitterness** — avoid in most cases. Bitter + bitter compounds.

**Texture** — light with light, heavy with heavy (safe). Contrast (light food + heavy wine, or vice versa) works only with care.

## 5. Wine styles → dish patterns

A practical map. For each wine style: what works well, what to avoid.

### Dry white (Sauvignon Blanc, dry Riesling, unoaked Chardonnay, Albariño, Vermentino, Soave, Pinot Grigio, Picpoul)
- Goat cheese; oysters; raw fish; sushi/sashimi; ceviche; light grilled white fish (sea bass, cod, sea bream); shellfish (mussels, clams, prawns); salads with vinaigrette; chicken without sauce; vegetable risotto; pesto pasta.
- Avoid: red meat, big creamy sauces, heavy braises.

### Aromatic / off-dry white (Gewürztraminer, off-dry Riesling, Viognier)
- Spicy Asian (Thai curry, Vietnamese, spicy Sichuan); Indian; spring rolls; brie / soft fresh cheese; foie gras (off-dry only, Sauternes is better); pâté.
- Avoid: very bitter foods.

### Rich oaked white (oaked Chardonnay, white Burgundy, Marsanne/Roussanne, white Rioja)
- Lobster; salmon in cream; roast chicken (especially with butter); cream-sauce pasta; mushroom dishes; smoked fish; rich poultry; veal in cream.
- Avoid: spicy heat (alcohol amplifies it); raw oysters (too rich).

### Sparkling wine (Champagne, Cava, Crémant, Franciacorta, English sparkling)
- Oysters; caviar; chicken liver pâté; salty fried food (fish & chips, fried chicken, tempura); brunch; salmon and caviar; smoked salmon; canapés; salted snacks; jamón ibérico; raw shellfish.
- Avoid: sweet desserts (use sweet wine instead). Pet Nat is the exception for funky cheese / fermented foods / earthy lentils.

### Dry rosé (Provence, Bandol, Tavel)
- Tomato salad; tuna; bouillabaisse; vegetable soup; charcuterie; light grilled fish; chilled summer dishes; goat cheese; Provençal cuisine.
- Avoid: heavy red meat, sweet desserts.

### Light red (Pinot Noir, Gamay/Beaujolais, Mencía, Dolcetto, lighter Sangiovese)
- Roast chicken; duck breast (Pinot Noir + duck is a canonical match); salmon (oily fish + light red works); pork loin; mushroom dishes; charcuterie; light pasta with tomato; venison loin; lighter cured cheeses; Boeuf Bourguignon.
- Avoid: very spicy food; dishes that overwhelm.

### Medium red (Sangiovese/Chianti, Tempranillo, Côtes du Rhône, Merlot, Cabernet Franc)
- Pizza (Sangiovese is the canonical pizza wine); pasta in tomato or meat ragù; pork belly, lamb shanks; mid-weight roasts; aged hard cheese; veal; mushroom pasta; eggplant parmesan.
- Avoid: very delicate fish; sweet desserts.

### Full-bodied red (Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah/Shiraz, Malbec, Nebbiolo/Barolo, Zinfandel, Bordeaux blends)
- Grilled red meat (steak, ribeye, T-bone — Cab/Malbec); BBQ (Zinfandel + ribs/sausage/bacon burger); slow-cooked rack of lamb (Napa Cab); game (Barolo + ossobuco, brasato); blue-veined and mature hard cheese; bistecca alla fiorentina; cured meats (salumi, jamón).
- Avoid: delicate fish, light vegetables, spicy heat.

### Sweet wine (Sauternes, late-harvest Riesling, Tokaji, Moscato d'Asti, Eiswein, Port)
- Foie gras (Sauternes); blue cheese (Sauternes / Port / Tokaji); caramelised desserts; fruit tarts; bitter dark chocolate (late-harvest Zinfandel); curry (Tokaji as bold contrast); pâté.
- Avoid: dry savoury dishes where there's no reason for sweetness.

### Vino passito / dessert wines (Italy)
- Spicy gorgonzola; cantucci biscuits; mixed cheese boards (covers fresh + aged in one wine).
- Avoid: dry savoury.

## 7. Pairing by region (when in doubt, follow the terroir)

- **France** — Bordeaux red with lamb / red meat ; Burgundy red (Pinot Noir) with duck / Boeuf Bourguignon ; Sancerre with goat cheese ; Sauternes with foie gras and Roquefort ; Champagne with everything salty ; Provence rosé with summer Mediterranean food.
- **Italy** — Barolo / Barbaresco with red meat / game ; Chianti with tomato pasta and pizza ; Vermentino with pesto and seafood salads ; Nero d'Avola with Sicilian melanzane ; Prosecco with Veneto antipasti ; Verdicchio with Adriatic fish ; Pecorino + Vino Nobile.
- **Spain** — Rioja Gran Reserva with lamb ; Albariño with Galician seafood ; Tempranillo with chorizo / patatas a la riojana ; Manzanilla & Fino with jamón ibérico, olives, almonds, prawns ; Cava with cocido madrileño ; Mencía with mushrooms.
- **Germany / Austria** — Spätburgunder (German Pinot Noir) with Sauerbraten and duck ; Riesling (dry) with pork, charcuterie ; Riesling (off-dry/Spätlese) with Thai / spicy ; Eiswein with foie gras and dessert.
- **Australia / New World** — Shiraz with BBQ / lamb / pepper-crusted steak ; Australian Chardonnay with grilled chicken / butter sauces ; Sauvignon Blanc (NZ) with asparagus, herb salads ; Pinot Noir with salmon.

## 8. How to use this skill (for the agent)

When asked "what wine should I serve with X?", do this walk:

1. Identify the **dominant flavour** in the dish (umami / fat / acid / sweet / salt / bitter / spice).
2. Identify the **dominant cooking method** (raw / grilled / braised / fried / poached).
3. Identify the **sauce**, if any. The sauce often outweighs the protein.
4. Apply the **intensity rule** — pick light/medium/full-bodied based on the dish's weight.
5. Apply the **acidity rule** — wine acid ≥ food acid.
6. Decide **concordance or contrast** (rich+rich? or rich+acidic?).
7. Cross-check **alcohol** (if spicy, lower) and **tannin** (if delicate, lower).
8. If the cuisine is regional, default to a wine from the **same region** unless a clear reason not to.
9. Output a *style* ("medium-bodied red, high acidity, low tannin, Mediterranean herbs") AND a *named example* from the canonical list in section 6.

When asked "what dish should I cook for this wine?", invert: take the wine's profile (body, acidity, tannin, alcohol, aroma) and pick the dish category that mirrors or contrasts as appropriate.
