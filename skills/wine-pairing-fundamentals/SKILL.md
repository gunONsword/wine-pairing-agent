# Wine Pairing — Fundamentals

The reasoning layer. Every category-specific pairing skill (meat, fish, cheese, pasta, dessert, vegetables, cuisine) sits on top of these rules. When in doubt, walk this skill first, then drill into the dish-specific one.

---

## 1. The two strategies

Every pairing is built on one of two ideas:

**Concordance (similarity, congruent).** Match characteristics. Rich dish + rich wine. Sweet dish + sweet wine. Herbaceous dish + herbaceous wine. Smoky dish + smoky wine. The wine *confirms and amplifies* the dish's dominant flavour.

**Contrast (contraposition, opposition).** Oppose characteristics so each balances the other. Acidic wine cuts fat. Sweet wine softens spicy heat. Tannic wine cuts protein/fat. Bubbles reset the palate after salt. The wine and dish *balance* each other.

Most professional pairings use one explicitly. A good agent should state which strategy it is using.

---

## 2. The intensity / weight rule (most important)

The wine's weight (body) and the dish's heaviness must match.

| Dish weight | Wine body |
|---|---|
| Light (salad, raw fish, steamed white fish) | Light-bodied, low alcohol, fresh, fruity |
| Medium (roast chicken, pork loin, light pasta) | Medium-bodied |
| Heavy (red meat, game, braised dishes, rich pasta) | Full-bodied, possibly tannic or oaked |

This is the **single most-cited rule** across every source. Violate it and either the wine overpowers the dish or vice versa.

A heuristic: if you can't lift a glass of the wine easily after eating, it's too heavy; if you barely notice the wine, it's too light.

---

## 3. The acidity rule

The wine should have **at least as much acidity** as the dish. If the dish is more acidic than the wine, the wine tastes flat / washed out.

Dishes high in acidity:
- Anything with tomato (pasta al pomodoro, pizza margherita, ratatouille)
- Anything with vinaigrette, lemon, citrus, vinegar
- Raw fish (sushi, sashimi, ceviche, carpaccio)
- Pickled / marinated foods
- Yogurt-based sauces

For these, raise the wine acidity. Sauvignon Blanc, Riesling, Albariño, Champagne, Chianti, Sangiovese, Pinot Grigio, Verdejo are all high-acid choices.

Bonus: high-acid wines also cleanse the palate against fat, cream, fried foods. So acid covers two needs at once.

---

## 4. The sweetness rule

**The wine must be sweeter than the food.** If the food is sweeter than the wine, the wine reads as sour or bitter.

Dessert pairings: the most common mistake is brut/extra-brut sparkling with a sweet dessert. The acidity of the brut is amplified by the dessert's sugar and the wine tastes harsh. Always go to Moscato d'Asti, late-harvest Riesling, Sauternes, Tokaji, Eiswein, vintage Port — anything explicitly sweet.

Savoury dishes with hidden sweetness (caramelised sauce, fruit reduction, sweet glaze, honey ham, BBQ sauce): you want a wine with at least a perception of sweetness. Off-dry Riesling, Gewürztraminer, or a ripe Chardonnay where alcohol reads as sweetness.

---

## 5. The tannin rule

Tannins bind to **protein and fat** and soften in the mouth.

| Tannin level | Works with | Avoid with |
|---|---|---|
| High (Cabernet Sauvignon, Nebbiolo, Syrah, Tannat, Aglianico, Sagrantino) | Grilled red meat, game, mature hard cheese, rich braises | Delicate fish, light vegetables, very bitter foods |
| Medium (Sangiovese, Tempranillo, Merlot, Malbec) | Pasta with meat sauce, pizza, pork roast, lamb, aged cheese | Spicy heat, sweet desserts |
| Low (Pinot Noir, Gamay, Mencía, Dolcetto) | Salmon, duck, roast chicken, mushrooms, charcuterie | Bigger steaks (wine will be overwhelmed) |
| Zero (white, rosé, sparkling) | Everything else | Tannin-binding mature cheese paradoxically often pairs poorly even with reds — go to dessert wine |

Bitter + bitter compound, never cancel. So high-tannin red + bitter green (artichoke, raw kale, endive) is a bad idea.

---

## 6. The salt rule

Salt distorts oaky whites (makes them taste odd), strips fruit from reds, and turns high-alcohol wines bitter.

Two reliable rescues for salty food:

**Sweet wine.** Sauternes + blue cheese (Roquefort, Stilton, Gorgonzola). Port + Stilton. Tokaji + cured ham.

**Sparkling.** Champagne, Cava, Crémant + fried salty food (fish & chips, fried chicken, tempura, salty fritto misto). The carbonation and yeasty acid emulate beer and reset the palate.

For briny seafood (oysters, sea urchin, raw clams): high-acid white. Champagne, Chablis, Muscadet, Albariño, Manzanilla. Avoid any oak.

---

## 7. The alcohol rule

High-alcohol wine (≥14.5 %) + spicy / chilli food = amplified heat. Avoid.

For spicy heat, drop alcohol and add sweetness. Off-dry Riesling, Gewürztraminer, Moscato d'Asti, Demi-sec Chenin.

Save high-alcohol wines (Zinfandel, Châteauneuf-du-Pape, Amarone, Primitivo, Shiraz) for braises, stews, hearty steaks, BBQ — dishes that need the structure.

---

## 8. The sauce / preparation rule

**Pair to the sauce or the cooking method, not just the protein.** Same chicken, three sauces, three different wines.

Cooking method cheat-sheet:
- Grilled / barbecued / smoked → smoky, mineral, or fumé wines (Rioja, Pinot Noir, Syrah, smoky Chardonnay)
- Braised / mijoté / slow-cooked → fresh, acidic wines that lift the dish (young Sangiovese, Gamay, Riesling)
- Boiled / poached / steamed → light reds (Gamay, Pinot Noir) or crisp simple whites (Sauvignon Blanc, Pinot Grigio)
- À feu vif (sharp crust, intense aromas, seared) → tannic reds (Chianti, Barolo, Cabernet)
- Fried → sparkling wines (the acidity + bubbles cut through grease)
- Raw → high-acid whites, Champagne, dry Riesling

Sauce cheat-sheet:
- Citrus / herb sauces → Sauvignon Blanc, Sancerre, Verdejo
- Cream / butter / mushroom sauces → oaked Chardonnay, Viognier, white Burgundy
- Tomato / pizza sauce → Sangiovese, Chianti, Barbera, Tempranillo
- Peppercorn / spice / curry → Shiraz, Syrah, Gewürztraminer, Riesling
- BBQ / smoky sauce → Zinfandel, Shiraz, Malbec
- Asian umami (soy, miso, fish sauce) → Pinot Noir, off-dry Riesling, Champagne

## 9. The terroir rule ("what grows together, goes together")

Regional cuisine + regional wine is rarely wrong. The vines were selected over centuries by farmers who ate the local food.

Iconic regional matches:
- Barolo / Barbaresco + Piedmontese game and ossobuco
- Chianti + Tuscan beef, pasta with meat ragù, pizza
- Pizzoccheri + Valtellina Superiore (Chiavennasca / Nebbiolo)
- Boeuf Bourguignon + Burgundy Pinot Noir
- Coq au vin + Burgundy red
- Sancerre + Crottin de Chavignol goat cheese
- Sauternes + Roquefort
- Rioja Gran Reserva + Castilian roast lamb
- Albariño + Galician seafood
- Tempranillo + chorizo, patatas a la riojana
- Manzanilla / Fino + jamón ibérico, olives, prawns
- Gewürztraminer + Munster
- Spätburgunder + Sauerbraten (German Pinot Noir + Rhineland pot roast)
- Riesling + Schweinshaxe
- Nero d'Avola + Sicilian pasta alla Norma

If you know nothing else about a dish, find its region and serve a wine from there.

## 10. The six elements framework (Wine Enthusiast)

A diagnostic checklist for any dish. Identify which elements dominate, then build the wine.

### 10.1 Fat
Wine has no fat. Three counter-moves: balance with **acid**, cut with **tannin**, or match richness with **alcohol/body**.
- Steak + Cabernet (tannin softens against fat, then fruit comes through)
- Fried chicken + Champagne (acid+bubbles)
- Pork belly + Riesling (acid cuts fat)

### 10.2 Acid
Wine acidity ≥ food acidity. For tomato dishes, raw fish, vinaigrettes. Mismatch → wine tastes flat.

### 10.3 Salt
Drives toward sweet wine (Sauternes + blue cheese) or sparkling (Champagne + salty fried).

### 10.4 Sweetness
Wine must be sweeter than the dessert. Light sweetness in savoury (fruit sauce, honey) needs a wine that reads sweet — oaked Chardonnay, late-harvest Riesling.

### 10.5 Bitterness
Avoid where possible. Bitter wine + bitter food compound. For bitter greens or artichoke, go to oily/buttery white or off-dry that wraps the bitterness.

### 10.6 Texture (body / weight)
Light with light, heavy with heavy. Safe. Adventurous: contrast (light food + heavy wine) — requires testing.

## 11. Service order in a multi-wine meal

When serving multiple wines through a meal:
- Light **before** heavy
- Young **before** old
- Fresh **before** tempered (cool wine before warmer wine)
- Dry **before** sweet
- White before red is *not* a real rule — weight is.

Spanish tradition (mulecarajonero):
- Blanco joven → blanco crianza → rosado → tinto joven → tinto envejecido

## 12. Service temperatures (often makes or breaks the pairing)

| Wine | Temperature |
|---|---|
| Sparkling (Champagne, Cava) | 8-10 °C — not icy, kills aroma |
| Light white / rosé | 8-10 °C |
| Rich/oaked white | 11-12 °C — not cold, the body needs warmth |
| Light red (Pinot Noir, Gamay) | 14-15 °C — slightly rafraîchi |
| Medium red | 16-17 °C |
| Full-bodied red | 17-18 °C — never room temperature in summer (>20 °C is too hot) |
| Sweet/dessert white | 9-11 °C |
| Vintage Port | 16-18 °C |

A wine served too cold tastes mute; too warm, alcohol dominates.

## 13. The myths to discard

❌ **"Red with meat, white with fish."** Depends on cut, preparation, and sauce. Oily fish (salmon, tuna, swordfish) takes a light red. Chicken in cream takes white.

❌ **"Rosé is a halfway compromise."** Rosé is its own category. A good Bandol rosé pairs with bouillabaisse better than most whites.

❌ **"Sparkling pairs with everything."** Dry sparkling fails against sweet desserts. Bubbles ≠ universal solvent.

❌ **"Wine must match the food's colour."** Old rule. Modern view: match the **weight and acidity**, not the colour.

❌ **"Only sommeliers can pair properly."** Pairing is a literacy. The rules above are enough for 90 % of decisions.

❌ **"Cheese always wants red."** White is usually better. See the cheese skill.

❌ **"Brut Champagne is for everything including dessert."** Brut is for savoury and salty. Use Demi-sec or Doux for dessert.

## 14. Decision walkthrough (use this when asked "what wine for X")

1. **Identify the dominant flavour** in the dish (umami / fat / acid / sweet / salt / bitter / spice).
2. **Identify the cooking method** (raw / grilled / braised / fried / poached / smoked).
3. **Identify the sauce**. The sauce often outweighs the protein.
4. **Apply the intensity rule.** Light/medium/full body based on dish weight.
5. **Apply the acidity rule.** Wine acid ≥ food acid.
6. **Decide concordance or contrast.** Rich+rich? Or rich+acidic to balance?
7. **Cross-check alcohol** (spicy → lower) and **tannin** (delicate → lower).
8. **Region.** If the dish is regional, default to a wine from the same region.
9. **Output**:
   - A *style* (e.g. "medium-bodied red, high acidity, low tannin, Mediterranean herbs"), and
   - One or two *named examples* (grape + region + 1 producer if relevant) from the category-specific skill.

For the inverse question ("what dish for this wine?") invert: take the wine's profile (body, acidity, tannin, alcohol, aroma) and pick the dish category that mirrors or contrasts as appropriate.

## 15. Quick reference card

| If the dish is… | Reach for… | Avoid… |
|---|---|---|
| Acidic (tomato, vinaigrette) | High-acid wine (Sangiovese, SB, Albariño) | Soft low-acid wines |
| Fatty (steak, pork belly) | Tannin (Cab, Nebbiolo) or acid (Riesling, Champagne) | Sweet low-acid wine |
| Salty (oysters, jamón, fries) | Sparkling, high-acid white, or sweet wine | Oaked heavy reds |
| Sweet (dessert) | Sweeter wine (Sauternes, Moscato, Port) | Brut sparkling, dry red |
| Spicy heat (chilli) | Off-dry Riesling, Gewürztraminer | High-alcohol reds |
| Bitter (artichoke, kale) | Buttery white, sparkling, off-dry | High-tannin red |
| Smoky / grilled | Smoky red (Rioja, Syrah) | Light fruity wines |
| Creamy / buttery | Oaked Chardonnay (concordance) or crisp acid (contrast) | Tannic red |
| Earthy / umami (mushrooms, truffle) | Pinot Noir, Nebbiolo, aged whites | Light fruity wines |
| Raw (sashimi, oysters) | Champagne, Chablis, Muscadet, dry Riesling | Anything oaked |
